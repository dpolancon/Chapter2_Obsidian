# 02B_Objectives_and_Contribution

## Objective

To reconstruct the theory of capacity utilization as a structurally mediated outcome of capitalist reproduction rather than as a given macroeconomic ratio or a residual left after trend estimation.

## Specific objectives

1. To restate Shaikh’s capacity-utilization problem as an unresolved theoretical and measurement problem.
2. To rework that problem through Okishio’s macroeconomic theory of decentralized accumulation, disproportionality, and crisis.
3. To articulate that reconstruction through Vidal’s theory of the internally divided firm.
4. To identify the transformation elasticity linking accumulation to productive-capacity growth as the key object through which utilization becomes empirically reconstructible.
5. To show how distribution enters that reconstruction by mediating the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity.
6. To examine how the reconstructed object appears in a canonical central case and in Chile as a peripheral formation.

## Contribution

The chapter contributes a macro-meso reconstruction of capacity utilization. It takes Shaikh’s unresolved measurement problem as its point of departure, reworks it through Okishio’s theory of decentralized accumulation and crisis, and articulates that reconstruction through Vidal’s theory of the firm. Capacity utilization is thereby respecified as a structurally mediated object: not a purely technical ratio, not a naturally realized benchmark, and not a free residual left after estimating output on capital.

The chapter’s central contribution is to relocate the problem in the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity. That transformation is governed by an elasticity, $\theta$, which becomes the key object of empirical identification. Because distribution mediates that transformation, the identification of utilization is endogenous to distribution from the start. Productive capacity must first be reconstructed through the transformation relation; only then can capacity utilization be recovered relative to it.

This reconstruction also has a comparative payoff. The chapter does not treat the peripheral case as a deviation from a norm realized in the center. It treats both cases as determinations of the same structural problem under distinct historical conditions. What differs is not whether utilization exists as an object, but how the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity is mediated across unequal positions in the world economy.

## Bounded contribution

The chapter does not provide a full theory of crisis, a complete reconstruction of dependency theory, or a full deployment of the profitability agenda. It does not attempt a complete world-historical reconstruction of center–periphery dynamics.

Its contribution is narrower and more precise: to rebuild the capacity-utilization problem across levels, to identify the transformation elasticity as the hinge of its empirical reconstruction, and to establish a bounded comparative framework through which the object can be reconstructed in the center and in the periphery.
