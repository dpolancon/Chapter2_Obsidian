# 02_Research_Problem

## Constitutional statement

Capacity utilization is not a settled empirical ratio but an unresolved theoretical object. In Shaikh’s account, it cannot be read directly from data without reconstructing the relation between accumulation and productive capacity under instability.

This chapter reformulates that problem. It links Okishio’s dynamics of decentralized accumulation to Vidal’s theory of the internally divided firm, treating utilization as a socially mediated outcome rather than a given.

The empirical task is bounded: to examine how this reconstructed object appears in a canonical central case and in Chile as a peripheral formation within a hierarchically organized world of states.

## Controlled expansion

Capacity utilization is typically treated as a given macroeconomic ratio or as a stable benchmark. This treatment obscures a prior problem: the object itself is not theoretically resolved. In Shaikh’s reconstruction, utilization cannot be read directly from data without reconstructing the relation between accumulation and productive capacity under instability.

This chapter takes that unresolved point as its starting problem. If utilization is not a transparent ratio, then it must be explained as an outcome. The difficulty is that existing accounts either remain at the macro level or treat firms as coherent optimizing units, leaving the internal organization of production undertheorized.

The problem is therefore to reconstruct capacity utilization across levels: to connect Shaikh’s macroeconomic identification problem with Okishio’s dynamics of decentralized accumulation and to articulate both through Vidal’s account of the internally divided firm. The empirical question follows directly: how does this reconstructed object appear in a canonical central case and in Chile as a peripheral formation within a hierarchically organized world of states?
## Programmatic Horizon

Capacity utilization appears in macroeconomic analysis as a measurable ratio, yet its status is unsettled. If capitalist reproduction is unstable, the relation between output and productive capacity cannot be assumed to reveal itself transparently in the data. Shaikh’s work makes this tension explicit but does not resolve how the object should be reconstructed.

The difficulty is not only empirical but theoretical. Macroeconomic accounts of accumulation and crisis often proceed without specifying how the units making decentralized decisions are internally organized. At the same time, firm-level accounts rarely connect these organizational contradictions to macroeconomic disequilibrium.

This chapter addresses that gap. It reconstructs capacity utilization as the outcome of contradictory organization across levels: the anarchy of decentralized accumulation (Okishio) articulated with the internally divided firm     


. The resulting problem is not how to measure a given ratio, but how to interpret its behavior across structurally unequal positions—here, a canonical central case and Chile as a peripheral formation.
