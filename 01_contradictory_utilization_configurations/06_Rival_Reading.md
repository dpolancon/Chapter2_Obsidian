# Rival reading

The strongest defender of the firm-level normal degree can concede that firms are internally contradictory while arguing that these contradictions affect implementation rather than the benchmark itself.

The defender’s position can be reconstructed as follows:

1. Firms may contain conflict, but hierarchy still aggregates conflict into a practical plan.
2. Capacity may be measured through different conventions, but these conventions approximate an underlying technical reality.
3. Utilization may vary tactically across plants and routines, but those variations remain deviations around a coherent long-run target.
4. Governance conflict may alter investment and payout decisions, but it does not eliminate the need for a normal utilization benchmark.
5. Measurement may be imperfect, but empirical approximation does not imply ontological incoherence.

# Reply

The reply is that contradiction reaches all three supports of the normal-degree concept:

- the subject presumed to choose the benchmark;
- the denominator against which utilization is measured;
- the temporal horizon over which the benchmark is supposed to hold.

The issue is not that firms fail to plan. The issue is that planning itself is fractured across labor process, plant-level operations, governance conflict, and measurement conventions. The replacement object is therefore not a better “normal degree,” but [[contradictory utilization configurations]].
