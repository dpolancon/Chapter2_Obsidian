| Anarchy of Production | The fundamental lack of centralized social planning where means of production are privately owned and investment decisions are uncoordinated. | Primary cause of systemic instability; individual capitalists make decisions based on subjective expectations without prior knowledge of total social demand. | Prevents the rigid conditions for "smooth" reproduction from being satisfied at any instant; actual growth is only a long-run average of fluctuations. | Uncoordinated private decisions lead to aggregate supply and demand mismatches, ensuring that macroeconomic disequilibrium arises and cumulatively worsens.          | "the condition that smooth reproduction is carried out in the capitalist system is not satisfied at any instant"                                                                                      | [1]    |
| Capital Accumulation  | Active financial and physical investments in additional production goods beyond the replacement of exhausted equipment.                       | Acts as the primary independent variable dictating total aggregate demand in the capitalist economy, often through a "multiplier effect."                     | Requires the rate of accumulation (g) to equal the growth rate of labor supply (n) to maintain constant unemployment and sustainability.               | Reactionary investment based on past profits or utilization generates positive feedback loops that drive the system away from equilibrium into booms or depressions. | "private capitalists make decisions on accumulation privately and dispersedly. As a result, a general disequilibrium arises, and the disequilibrium must be cumulative."                              | [1]    |
| Capacity Utilization  | The ratio between actual production determined by demand and the normal production capacity of existing capital equipment.                    | Functions as the primary behavioral signal for investment; capitalists adjust future accumulation based on current utilization levels.                        | A stable, 100% (normal) utilization rate is a requirement for equilibrium, but in reality, it is strictly endogenous and highly unstable.              | High utilization triggers a surge in investment demand, which further increases aggregate demand and utilization, creating a runaway upward cumulative process.      | "[W]e could consider the size of accumulation demand an increasing function of the utilization rate in the sector."                                                                                   | [1]    |
| Economic Crisis       | A violent reversal and restoration process that forcibly halts a runaway boom or corrects a downward spiral.                                  | Necessary reversal required because the system cannot survive indefinite cumulative disequilibrium or the total absorption of the labor reserve.              | Serves as the mechanism that "yanks" the economy back toward the equilibrium trajectory after it has diverged too far.                                 | Triggered by overproduction in consumption goods due to restricted wages, labor shortages, or bottlenecks in the production goods sector.                            | "the capitalist system... must continuously rely on these violent reversals to clear out obsolete capital, discipline labor, and forcibly recreate the conditions necessary for capital accumulation" | [1]    |
| Productive Capacity   | The physical state of potential output achievable using the production goods currently owned by capitalists.                                  | Acts as a hard physical constraint on short-term growth; if demand exceeds maximum capacity, physical bottlenecks occur.                                      | In equilibrium, capacity must grow in proportion with accumulation and labor supply to avoid shortages or permanent idle capacity.                     | A bottleneck arises when runaway accumulation hits the maximum capacity of the production goods sector, derailing expansion and triggering a crisis.                 | "if surging aggregate demand requires a production level that exceeds the maximum production capacity of the sectors, actual production cannot simply rise to meet the demand."                       | [1]    |
