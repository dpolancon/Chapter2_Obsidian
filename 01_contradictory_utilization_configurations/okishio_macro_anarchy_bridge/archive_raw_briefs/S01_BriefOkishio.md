# The Theory of Accumulation: A Marxian Approach to the Dynamics of Capitalist Economy

## Executive Summary

This document synthesizes the foundational theories of Nobuo Okishio regarding the accumulation and dynamics of a capitalist economy. Based on a Marxian framework, the analysis defines capitalism as a transitional social system characterized by specific "production relations" that must correspond to the level of "productive forces." The central thesis posits that profit is not a result of technology or market luck but is fundamentally rooted in **surplus labor**, where the real wage rate is kept below the level of labor productivity.

Critically, Okishio argues against mainstream "vulgar" economic theories, asserting that the real wage rate is not determined in the labor market, but is instead a product of equilibrium in the commodity markets—driven primarily by the **accumulation demand** of capitalists. This demand creates a cumulative process of disequilibrium, necessitating cyclical economic crises to forcibly restore equilibrium. The document outlines the structural inevitability of these cycles and the eventual transition of capitalism as productive forces outgrow the constraints of private ownership.

## I. Fundamental Structure of the Capitalist Economy

The capitalist system is analyzed not as a perpetual state of nature but as a specific, historical form of social organization. Its function is determined by the interplay between productive forces and production relations.

### 1. Productive Forces and Production Relations

- **Productive Forces:** Defined as the human ability to control nature through conscious, social labor utilizing manufactured tools. This force must develop continually for the survival of the species as nature undergoes irreversible changes.
- **Production Relations:** The social arrangements regarding the ownership of the means of production, the division of labor, and the distribution of products.
- **The Fundamental Relation:** In any society, the **ownership of the means of production** is the fundamental relation that determines who works, who makes decisions, and how products are distributed. In capitalism, this ownership is private and dispersive.

### 2. The Admissible Range of Productive Forces

Capitalist production relations can only exist within a specific range of productive forces:

- **The Lower Limit:** Labor productivity must be high enough to produce a surplus beyond the workers' subsistence, and production goods must require enough labor to prevent workers from easily owning their own means of production.
- **The Upper Limit:** Capitalism becomes unstable when productive forces reach a level where:
    - Labor productivity is so high it causes constant overproduction or massive unemployment.
    - The minimum funds required for production exceed private financing capabilities (requiring state intervention).
    - The ability to process information allows for unified social control, making private, "anarchic" decision-making obsolete.

## II. The Theory of Profit and Surplus Labor

Okishio rejects "vulgar" explanations for profit—such as manager wages, capital productivity, or "rewards" for innovation—and identifies its source through a rigorous examination of commodity production and wage labor.

### 1. Necessary Social Conditions for Profit

For profit to exist, three conditions must be satisfied:

- **Commodity Production:** Social division of labor exists alongside private ownership, requiring products to be exchanged for money to be "socially" validated.
- **Wage Labor:** Laborers must be "free" in two senses: free to sell their labor power and "free" (deprived) of the means of production, forcing them to sell their labor to survive.
- **Surplus Labor:** Laborers must work longer hours than are required to produce the value of the goods they consume.

### 2. The Mathematical "Surplus Condition"

Profit existence (Profit > 0) requires that: 1 - Rt_2 > 0

- **R** **(Real Wage Rate):** The quantity of consumption goods a laborer receives per unit of labor.
- **t_2** **(Bestowed Labor):** The total direct and indirect labor required to produce one unit of consumption goods.
- **The Result:** This inequality implies that the real wage rate must be lower than labor productivity (R < 1/t_2). If the real wage rate rises to meet productivity, profit disappears.

## III. Determination and Dynamics of the Real Wage Rate

A central contribution of the text is the critique of mainstream wage theories and the proposal of a "Commodity-Market Theory" of wages.

### 1. Rejection of Mainstream Theories

- **Marginal Productivity Theory:** Okishio argues this is merely a labor demand function for capitalists and cannot determine the wage rate itself. It falsely assumes full employment (N = Ns).
- **Labor-Market Theory:** This theory fails to distinguish between the **money wage rate** and the **real wage rate**. Okishio notes that even if money wages fall during a recession, the real wage may _rise_ if commodity prices fall faster due to overproduction.

### 2. The Commodity-Market Theory

The real wage rate is temporarily determined by the equilibrium of the commodity markets. If demand in the commodity market is low, the real wage rate tends to rise (prices fall relative to wages), leading capitalists to reduce production and employment.

- **Keynesian Critique:** While Keynes recognized that wages are determined by commodity demand, he viewed this as a "natural law." Okishio argues this is a result of capitalist production relations where employment decisions are based on profit-seeking.

### 3. The Admissible Range of Wages (R_{min} to R_{max})

For capitalism to reproduce itself, the real wage (R) must stay within a specific range:

- **R_{min}** **(Lower Limit):** Determined by physiological and social subsistence needs. If wages fall below this, laborers cannot reappear in the market.
- **R_{max}** **(Upper Limit):** Wages cannot be so high that workers can save enough to buy their own means of production or that the exploitation rate falls below the "normal" level required to incentivize capitalist production.

## IV. The Role of Accumulation Demand

The "Accumulation Demand" (capitalists' demand for new production goods) is the primary engine of the capitalist economy and the source of its inherent instability.

### 1. Impact on Production and Wages

- **Increased Accumulation Demand:** When capitalists increase investment, it raises the price of production and consumption goods relative to money wages. This **lowers the real wage rate** and increases the profit rate, encouraging further production.
- **The Cumulative Process:** Disequilibrium in capitalism is cumulative. An upward trend in demand accelerates production and lowers real wages, while a downward trend (decreased demand) leads to falling prices, rising real wages, and further production cuts.

### 2. Inevitability of Crisis

Because the accumulation demand is based on private, dispersive decisions, it leads to "anarchic" production. The cumulative process of disequilibrium must eventually be reversed through a **violent equilibration**—an economic crisis.

- **The Cycle:** Upward cumulative disequilibrium \rightarrow Halt \rightarrow Crisis \rightarrow Downward cumulative disequilibrium \rightarrow Reversal \rightarrow Recovery.

## V. Transition and Contemporary Issues

The document concludes by addressing the evolution of capitalism into "State-Monopoly Capitalism" and its eventual transformation.

### 1. State-Monopoly Capitalism

Okishio questions whether state intervention can avoid crises. He argues that while the state can offset decreases in accumulation demand through expenditures (often military), it does not resolve the fundamental contradiction of private ownership and anarchic production.

- **Military Expenditure:** This plays a dual role: creating demand and providing the force necessary to secure resources and exploit labor globally.

### 2. The Transformation of the System

As productive forces develop, they eventually act as "fetters" on production. The text identifies several indicators of this transition:

- Deepening difficulty in finding markets.
- The tendency for the profit rate to fall as productivity increases.
- The necessity of public funds to finance massive infrastructure.
- Environmental pollution and the inability of private capital to manage global nature.
- The socialization of information-processing.

**Key Insight:** "At a certain stage of development, the material productive forces of society come into conflict with the existing relations of production... From forms of development of productive forces, these relations turn into their fetters, and an era of social revolution begins." — _Karl Marx (quoted in context)_
