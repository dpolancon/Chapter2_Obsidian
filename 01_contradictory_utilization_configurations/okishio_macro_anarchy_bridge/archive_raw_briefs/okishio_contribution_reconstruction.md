

**1. Decentralized Accumulation** Okishio’s core argument centers on the fact that the capitalist economy lacks central planning, operating instead under an "anarchy" of production. Because the means of production are privately owned, **individual capitalists make accumulation (investment) decisions dispersedly and privately, relying entirely on subjective expectations rather than prior knowledge of total social demand**. Crucially, capitalists must commit to their accumulation demand _before_ their profits are actually realized. Because these proactive, uncoordinated investment decisions dictate total social demand, aggregate production and aggregate demand fundamentally fail to match, guaranteeing that a macroeconomic disequilibrium arises across the entire society.

**2. Disproportionality and Disequilibrium** Contrary to the view that market mechanisms naturally self-correct the economy, Okishio argues that **the price mechanism acts as a positive feedback loop that actively worsens disproportionality**. When a general disequilibrium occurs—such as excess demand in the market—prices and profit rates rise, which private capitalists interpret as a signal to further increase their accumulation demand. Because capitalists react to these signals by selfishly trying to recover their own local equilibrium without regard for the macroeconomic balance, their simultaneous investments inject massive new aggregate demand into the system. **This ensures that disequilibrium does not resolve itself, but instead violently cumulates**—spiraling upward into runaway booms of excess demand or downward into severe depressions of general overproduction.

**4. The Impossibility of Smooth Reproduction** Okishio demonstrates that "smooth" extended reproduction is practically impossible because it requires highly rigid and fragile mathematical conditions to be simultaneously met. Specifically, there must be a perfect, mathematically precise "composition of production" (the ratio between the production goods and consumption goods sectors), alongside perfect profit realization and balanced real wages. However, because the system relies on the anarchic, private decisions of dispersed capitalists, it is "extremely hard to satisfy" these strict proportional relationships. Because these delicate conditions are **"almost always disturbed in an anarchic capitalist system,"** Okishio famously concludes that **"the condition that smooth reproduction is carried out in the capitalist system is not satisfied at any instant"**. Instead, the "equilibrium" trajectory acts merely as a theoretical benchmark; actual steady growth is never experienced smoothly, but is only achieved "in the long run and on average" through the violent corrections of continuous business cycles and crises.
