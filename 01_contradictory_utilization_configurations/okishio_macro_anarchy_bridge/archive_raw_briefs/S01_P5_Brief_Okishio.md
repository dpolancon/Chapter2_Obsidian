

# Analysis of Okishio’s Critique of Market Feedback and Macroeconomic Disequilibrium

## Executive Summary

The economic framework developed by Okishio challenges the conventional "vulgar view" of market self-regulation. The central thesis is that the capitalist market mechanism, characterized by the "anarchy of production," lacks a stabilizing negative feedback loop. Instead, market signals—such as high prices and profit rates—function as positive feedback that drives the macroeconomy toward cumulative disequilibrium. Because individual capitalists make investment decisions based on private profit motives and past market signals without centralized _ex-ante_ planning, their collective attempts to optimize local outcomes paradoxically amplify social disproportionality, suppress the long-term average profit rate, and trigger crises of overproduction.

## The Breakdown of the "Invisible Hand"

Okishio identifies a fundamental failure in the standard price adjustment mechanism when applied to the macroeconomy. While the "invisible hand" may resolve imbalances in isolated commodity markets, it fails to achieve social equilibrium across all sectors.

### Negative vs. Positive Feedback

- **Microeconomic (Negative Feedback):** In a single market, such as sugar, a shortage raises prices. This higher price discourages consumption and encourages production, eventually restoring balance.
- **Macroeconomic (Positive Feedback):** When a general disequilibrium affects the entire economy, high prices and profit rates do not signal a need for caution. Instead, they encourage a surge in "accumulation demand" (investment in new production goods), which further increases aggregate demand, driving the system further from equilibrium.

### The Problem of "Vulgar" Assumptions

The conventional view assumes that price changes automatically guide capitalists toward socially appropriate adjustments. Okishio argues this logic collapses because capitalists act reactively, basing future production on the market signals of the previous period without knowing future social demand.

## The Anarchy of Production and Accumulation

The "anarchy of production" is the root cause of macroeconomic instability. It arises from the private ownership of the means of production and the absence of centralized social planning.

### Characteristics of Anarchic Production

1. **Private Optimization:** Decisions are made by individual firms seeking to maximize private profit rather than social equilibrium.
2. **Information Deficit:** Capitalists must decide on production capacity and investment "without knowing social demand in advance."
3. **Ex-Post Coordination:** Coordination only occurs after the fact through market signals, which are often misinterpreted or lead to delayed, reactionary responses.

### Reactionary Accumulation Demand

Because there is no _ex-ante_ plan, capitalists use the profit rate and capacity utilization rate of the previous period as their primary guides. A high profit rate in one period triggers an aggressive increase in investment for the next. When all capitalists react to these same signals simultaneously, the uncoordinated surge in demand creates a runaway upward spiral or a "boom" that inevitably leads to a crash.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Paradoxes of Private Optimization

Okishio highlights several instances where the rational, profit-seeking intentions of individual firms lead to destructive collective outcomes.

|   |   |
|---|---|
|Private Intention (The Firm)|Macroeconomic Outcome (The Society)|
|**Recovering Equilibrium:** Increasing investment to capture profits during periods of high demand.|**Amplifying Disequilibrium:** Collective investment injections create massive new demand, causing the disequilibrium to violently cumulate.|
|**Maximizing Profit via Wage Suppression:** Keeping real wages low relative to productivity to increase exploitation.|**General Overproduction:** Restricted consumption power limits the market; expanded capacity eventually exceeds the masses' ability to consume, causing a crisis.|
|**Adopting New Technology:** Introducing labor-saving tech to cut costs and gain "extraordinary profits" over competitors.|**Falling Profit Rates:** As the technology becomes the social standard, the "organic composition of production" (ratio of dead labor to living labor) rises, lowering the average profit rate across the economy.|

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## The Role of Profit as a Driving Force

In Okishio’s framework, profitability is not merely a goal but the ultimate criterion for the survival of the capitalist.

- **The Survival Mandate:** If a capitalist abandons the pursuit of profit, they lose their capacity to operate and will eventually lose their means of production to competitors.
- **The Catalyst for Innovation:** Competition forces the adoption of new technologies. While intended to provide a private edge, this constant drive for productivity improvements fuels the overall accumulation of capital and shifts the economic structure.
- **The Driver of Investment Levels:** Production and sales are strictly means to an end. Capitalists will only purchase labor and operate machinery if they expect a satisfactory profit. This makes the entire stability of the social reproduction process dependent on volatile profit expectations.

## Conclusion: Inevitable Disequilibrium

The synthesis of individual, disconnected private decisions guarantees that the economy will frequently deviate from the proportions required for steady growth. Because private capitalists do not—and cannot—make decisions based on the adjustment needs of the whole society, their "locally optimized" reactions to market signals ensure that imbalances do not gently correct but rather cumulate into systemic instability. The "anarchic method" of production ensures that a gap between production capacity and social demand is not an accident, but an inevitability of the system.
