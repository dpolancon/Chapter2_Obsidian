# The Theory of Accumulation: A Marxian Analysis of Capitalist Dynamics

## Executive Summary

This briefing document synthesizes the economic theories of Nobuo Okishio, specifically focusing on the mechanisms of capital accumulation, the determination of real wages, and the inevitability of economic crises. The analysis explores the capitalist economy as a transitional social form defined by a fundamental contradiction: the conflict between socialized production and private ownership.

**Critical Takeaways:**

- **The Social Basis of Profit:** Profit is not a technological or natural byproduct of capital but a social phenomenon rooted in the exploitation of surplus labor. It exists only when the real wage rate is kept below labor productivity.
- **The Commodity-Market Theory of Wages:** Contrary to mainstream views, the real wage rate is primarily determined by equilibrium conditions in the commodity market rather than the labor market.
- **Impossibility of Smooth Reproduction:** Because capitalist production is "anarchic" (laced with private, uncoordinated decisions), "smooth reproduction"—or a steady, proportional growth path—is theoretically impossible to maintain at any given instant.
- **Inevitability of Crisis:** The capitalist system does not naturally gravitate toward equilibrium. Instead, it moves through cumulative processes of disequilibrium. Proportionality is only achieved in the long run through "violent reversals"—economic crises and business cycles that forcefully correct imbalances.
- **State-Monopoly Capitalism:** Modern state intervention and military expenditures serve to delay or alter the form of crises but cannot resolve the system's fundamental contradictions.

## 1. Fundamental Structure: Productive Forces and Production Relations

The capitalist system is characterized by a specific relationship between the "productive force" (humanity's ability to control nature) and "production relations" (the social structure governing production).

### 1.1 Productive Force

Production is the fundamental condition for human existence. Human production is distinct due to its conscious nature, use of manufactured tools, and social cooperation. The "productive force" must develop for humanity to survive irreversible changes in nature.

### 1.2 Production Relations

The core of any society is the ownership of the means of production. In a capitalist system:

- **Private Ownership:** Capitalists exclusively and dispersedly own the means of production.
- **Wage Labor:** Direct laborers are excluded from ownership and must sell their labor power to survive.
- **Anarchy of Production:** Decisions are made privately and based on profit-seeking, leading to a lack of social coordination.

### 1.3 Compatibility Limits

Capitalist relations can only function within a specific range of productive forces:

- **Lower Limit:** Productivity must be high enough to create a surplus beyond the laborers' reproduction needs and require significant capital investment to exclude laborers from self-employment.
- **Upper Limit:** When productivity becomes so high that private funding cannot manage it, or when production impacts nature globally (making commodity-based decisions dangerous), the capitalist form becomes a "fetter" on the productive force.

## 2. The Conditions for Profit Existence

Okishio challenges "vulgar" economic views (e.g., profit as a reward for risk, management wages, or capital productivity) by identifying the social requirements for profit.

### 2.1 Essential Social Conditions

For profit to exist, three conditions must be satisfied:

1. **Dominance of Commodity Production:** Products must be exchanged via money, requiring a social division of labor and private ownership.
2. **Commoditization of Labor Power:** Laborers must be "free" in two senses: personally free to sell labor and "free" (deprived) of the means of production.
3. **Existence of Surplus Labor:** Laborers must work longer hours than the time required to produce the consumption goods they receive as wages.

### 2.2 The "Surplus Condition"

Profit is mathematically dependent on the **Surplus Condition**:

- The real wage rate (R) must be lower than labor productivity (1/t_2).
- If R exceeds 1/t_2, the rate of exploitation becomes zero or negative, and profit disappears.
- **Mechanism:** Capitalists only hire labor when they expect an exploitation rate that yields a "normal" profit. This unequal power dynamic—where the laborer’s life depends on the capitalist’s decision—is the ultimate guarantee of surplus labor.

## 3. Real Wage Rate Determination

A central component of Okishio’s theory is the critique of how real wages are determined and the distinction between money wages and real wages.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Theory Type|Core Argument|Okishio’s Critique|
|**Reproduction Cost**|Wages gravitate toward the cost of sustaining the laborer.|Valid as a long-term center of gravity, but fails to explain short-term cyclical shifts.|
|**Labor-Market**|Wages are determined by supply/demand of labor.|Incorrectly conflates money wages with real wages; a labor shortage may raise money wages, but if commodity prices rise faster, real wages fall.|
|**Marginal Productivity**|Wages equal the marginal product of labor.|Fails to determine wages; it merely describes a capitalist's profit-maximizing employment level for a _given_ wage.|
|**Commodity-Market**|Wages are determined by supply/demand in the commodity market.|**Preferred View.** Capitalists adjust production based on commodity demand. Real wages are a byproduct of the price-wage relationship needed to clear markets.|

### 3.1 The Role of the Commodity Market

In a capitalist society, the economy seeks a **temporary equilibrium** in the commodity market. Employment and real wages are determined by the demand for production goods (investment) and consumption goods.

- An increase in **accumulation demand** (investment) by capitalists typically lowers the real wage rate by driving up commodity prices relative to money wages.
- This process reveals the class nature of the system: the rate of exploitation is adjusted via the market to provide the surplus labor required to fulfill capitalist demand.

## 4. Accumulation, Equilibrium, and Crisis

Okishio provides an incisive critique of "smooth reproducibility," arguing that the capitalist system is inherently unstable.

### 4.1 The Myth of Smooth Reproduction

"Smooth reproduction" refers to a state where sectors grow in perfect proportion. Okishio argues this is **impossible** at any given moment because:

- Investment decisions are private and uncoordinated ("Anarchy").
- The rigid proportional requirements for sectoral balance cannot be met by spontaneous market activity.

### 4.2 The Equilibrium Accumulation Trajectory

Okishio defines an "equilibrium trajectory" not as a path the economy _actually_ takes, but as a theoretical benchmark.

- **Constant Divergence:** The actual economy always moves away from this path in cumulative upward (boom) or downward (depression) processes.
- **Violent Reversals:** Because the system is self-reinforcing in its imbalances (e.g., high demand leads to more investment, further increasing demand), it cannot self-correct smoothly. It requires **economic crises** to forcefully destroy capital and reset the system toward the equilibrium axis.

### 4.3 The Business Cycle

The cycle follows a logical progression:

1. **Upward Cumulative Process:** High demand \rightarrow high profit \rightarrow high investment \rightarrow further demand.
2. **Halt and Crisis:** The process hits a limit (e.g., falling exploitation rates or market saturation).
3. **Downward Cumulative Process:** Falling demand \rightarrow losses \rightarrow scrapped equipment \rightarrow unemployment.
4. **Turnover:** Forced corrections (scrapping old tech, lowering wages) eventually restore the profit rate, initiating a new cycle.

## 5. Modern State-Monopoly Capitalism

Okishio’s analysis extends to the role of the state in contemporary capitalism, particularly in avoiding or mitigating crises.

### 5.1 Can the State Avoid Crisis?

While state intervention can shorten a downward cumulative process (depression) by increasing expenditure, it does not resolve the system's fundamental contradictions:

- The state does not change the private decision-making mechanism of investment.
- If the state blocks the "cleansing" effect of a crisis (e.g., preventing the scrapping of old technology), the economy may face stagnation or "parasitic" tendencies.

### 5.2 The Necessity of Military Expenditure

Military spending serves two roles in state-monopoly capitalism:

1. **Economic:** It provides a massive, reliable source of demand to offset decreases in private accumulation demand.
2. **Political:** It provides the "military power" necessary to monopolize global resources, oppress foreign populations, and discipline the domestic working class.

### 5.3 Limits of Reform

Okishio argues that the state remains an organ of the monopoly capitalist class. Even if labor representatives enter government, they cannot change the system's foundations without a fundamental transfer of power, as the "shell" of private property relations remains the governing logic of the economy.
