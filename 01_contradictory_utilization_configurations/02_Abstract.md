# Abstract

This essay argues that the concept of a firm-level normal degree of capacity utilization rests on a hidden and untenable assumption: that the capitalist firm operates as a unified decision-making body capable of orienting production around a coherent utilization benchmark. Drawing on heterodox macroeconomics, labor-process theory, industrial operations, and corporate governance, the paper shows that this assumption breaks down once the firm is treated as a contradictory social organization.

At the shop-floor level, utilization is shaped by struggles over control, skill, and routine reproduction. At the plant level, it is mediated by bottlenecks, tactical slack, and product-mix complexity. At the corporate level, it is conditioned by strategic conflict, shareholder heterogeneity, and financialized time horizons. The denominator itself—capacity—is not a neutral technical magnitude but a contested managerial, accounting, and organizational construction.

Building on accounts of labor management as divided between the extraction of disciplined manual effort and the mobilization of workers’ cognitive capacities, the essay proposes replacing the notion of a firm-wide normal degree with the concept of [[contradictory utilization configurations]]. This shift reframes debates in post-Keynesian and Marxian macroeconomics, the theory of the firm, and the measurement of productive slack.
