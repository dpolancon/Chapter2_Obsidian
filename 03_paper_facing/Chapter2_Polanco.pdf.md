# Chapter 2: Capacity Utilization as a Structurally Mediated Object


## Abstract 

This essay argues that the concept of a firm-level normal degree of capacity utilization rests on a hidden and untenable assumption: that the capitalist firm operates as a unified body of decision-making capable of orienting production around a coherent utilization benchmark. Once the firm is analyzed as a contradictory social organization rather than as a unified production function, that assumption breaks down.

## 1. Introduction 

This paper argues that the concept of a firm-level normal degree of capacity utilization rests on a hidden and untenable assumption: that the capitalist firm operates as a unified body of decision-making capable of orienting production around a coherent utilization benchmark. Once the firm is analyzed as a contradictory social organization rather than as a unified production function, that assumption breaks down.

Most debates on capacity utilization—whether in post-Keynesian or Marxian traditions—take for granted that firms can define, pursue, and adjust around a meaningful _“normal”_ rate. Even when the normal rate is treated as demand-endogenous rather than technologically fixed (Nikiforos, 2013), the firm remains implicitly modeled as a sufficiently coherent actor capable of aligning production, labor utilization, and capacity around a stable benchmark. The problem is not simply whether the normal rate is exogenous or endogenous. It is whether the firm organizes its objectives around a _desired normal rate of capacity utilization_ at all.

The essay presented in this chapters shows that it cannot. Drawing together labor-process theory, industrial operations, and corporate governance literature, it demonstrates that capacity utilization is not the expression of a unified plan but the outcome of contradictory organizational processes of a internally divided firm. At the shop-floor level, utilization is shaped by routine struggles over control, skill, and the organization of work. At the plant level, it is mediated by bottlenecks, tactical slack, and product-mix complexity. At the corporate level, it is conditioned by strategic conflict of shareholder value orientation mediated by financialized time horizons. Across these levels, capacity itself is not a neutral technical magnitude, neither exogenous determinated as argued by the classical-keynesian tradition (Kurz, 1986), but a contested managerial and accounting construction by conflicting insterest at multiples scales (Vidal, 2022), while its utilization migth be absolutly neglacted (Singh et. al, 2022), or understood through a wide an heterogenous convetions (United States General Accounting Office [GAO], 1978; Lavoie, 2004) .  

The theoretical underpinnings of my arguments is founded in a reformulation of the canonical approach to labor-studies. Reformulating and contesting Braverman (1974) seminal work on de-skilling, Vidal (2022) emphasizes that capitalist management is not organized around a single imperative of control, rather on the contradictions of labour managment on the need to secure disciplined labor effort and mobilize workers’ cognitive capacities for flexibility, coordination, and learning. This contradiction --- which have intensified through the development of capitalism during its post-Fordist era --- means that the firm cannot be treated as a unified decision-making subject. Instead, it operates as argued by Coase (1937) as a planning apparatus which conditions of existence are rooted in avoiding transaction costs. However, what cannot be understood within the narrow scope of neoclassical theory is the internally divided nature of labour managment which imposes a field of operation within the firm which is inherently fractured by the conflicting logics of managment and labour (Vidal, 2022). Not only at the shoop floor, but across the multiples scales of managment, in which different organizational levels and temporal horizons pull in different directions. Consequently, planned excess capacity at the firm level as a buffer to face uncertain demand conditions (Steindl, 1952), cannot be understood as the outcome of as a singular atomized entity pursuing a desired rate of capacity utilization (Ciccone, 1986; Skott, 2012; Nikiforos, 2013), rather, here is proposed that a more sound understanding is to interpret it as a buffer to the sources of dysfunctionality emerging from the fractures and contradictions of labour managment.  

This shift has direct implications for utilization theory. If the firm is internally divided, then capacity utilization cannot be interpreted as the realization of a coherent firm-wide target. It becomes the unstable outcome of what calls _contradictory utilization configurations_: heterogenous organizational settlements that combine labor discipline, workflow coordination, managerial discretion, and governance pressures at multiple scales, in different often incoherent and inconsistent forms. In one regime, firms prioritize standardized through output and manual effort; in another, they rely more heavily on worker problem-solving and learning; in most cases, they reproduce hybrid arrangements were managers settle for _"good-enough"_  output (Vidal, 2022), which allows to identify the institutionalization at firm level of mediocre sufficiency, which in turn  becomes a source of fragility and systemic dysfunctionality through its scalation to the aggregats, and projected to a dynamical setting.  

From a growth-and-distribution perspective, this implies that the _“normal”_ degree of utilization cannot serve as proposed by Nikiforos (2013) as a micro-foundational anchor to identify its endogeneity to demand. However, it can serve as a benchmark to identify the path of smooth reproduction under conditions of proportional expanded accumulation, which Okishio ([1964] 2022) defined as the path of equilibrium accumulation trajectory. \footnote{The literature have also coined for Okishio's _equilibrium_ trajectory  as the "golden path" of capitalist accumulation (Bergman, 2009) or the "smooth reproduction" of the capitalist mode of production (Basu, 2022), in this essay I use them interchangably as synonyms}. This is the approach taken in this essay, which, also place itself as a contribution to comparative and analyitical political economy literature, identifying the effects on the path of smooth reproduction of a binding external constraint on demand and accumulation inspired in the intervention of Kaldor (1959) on the problems of economic development in Chile. Therefore, utilization depends on how the labor process reproduces itself under conditions of contradiction, and on how distributive claims—within production and its multiple scale of self-governance—shape the temporal horizon of accumulation. Stable and high utilization is therefore not a generic property of firms but an  institutional outcome, contingent on specific combinations of demand conditions, labor relations, and organizational forms, governement expenditures, and differential integration to the world market. 

Finally, the argument re frames the relation between the firm and the market. Marxist Political Economy within the research avenue placing its level of abstraction solely in the value form, accurately have theorized the two-fold modes of domination within the capitalist system: the direct control of labour at the level of the firm as form of apperance of subordination from workers to capitalists on the one hand, and the impersonal domination of capital as a social relation that subordinates workers and capitalist alike at the site of the market caused by the contradiction carried by commodities due the dislocation of their use and the substance of its value, where the phenomena of commodity fetishism takes place (Basu, 2022). However, here is argued that the anarchic character of  markets under the capitalist mode of production does not disappear inside the firm; it is reorganized. Firms do not overcome market anarchy; they internalize it through constrained planning shaped by uncertainty and contradictory interests across organizational scales. The site of theorizatin for political economy is therefore not a firm-level normal degree of capacity utilization, but the historically specific configuration of contradictory utilization configurations through which production is organized and reproduced.


##  2. Stagnation, Contradiction, and the Configurations of Capacity Utilization

Capitalist production does not reproduce itself smoothly. The growth of capital and the growth of capacity unfold at different rhythms, governed by different determinants, and there is no automatic mechanism bringing them into balance. When capital accumulates faster than capacity expands, demand pressure mounts on existing capacity and prices rise. When capacity expands faster than capital is renewed, idle equipment accumulates and pressure mounts on profitability. The two rhythms can synchronize, but only momentarily. What follows is divergence in one direction or the other, and a new round of adjustment.

This is the structural finding established through Chapter 1. Balanced reproduction is a theoretical benchmark, not a state toward which capitalist production tends. The economy moves through cycles of overshooting and undershooting, with no internal mechanism producing convergence. The supply side — the conditions under which means of production are accumulated, deployed, and renewed — generates instability rather than damping it. Investment decisions made in one period produce capacity that comes online in subsequent periods, by which time the conditions that motivated those decisions have already shifted. The lag is not incidental. It is constitutive of how capitalist accumulation works.

The implication is uncomfortable for any theory that treats firms as the source of economic stabilization. If the problem were temporary — a shock followed by adjustment — firms could in principle adapt. But the problem is structural. Each adjustment generates new imbalances. Each correction overshoots. Stabilization, if it is to occur, must come from somewhere outside the firm's own decision horizon. It must come from coordination across firms, or from regulation external to the firm, or from the absorption of imbalance into buffers that the firm cannot construct alone.

Coordination across firms is what markets are supposed to provide. Here the difficulty deepens. Markets coordinate through prices, and prices respond to the imbalances themselves. By the time the price signal reaches the firm, the imbalance has already been produced; by the time the firm responds, the signal has changed. The market does not solve the coordination problem. It transmits it, with delay, and at a distance from the conditions that generated it. External regulation requires institutional capacity that exists only contingently — and where it exists, it operates at scales the firm cannot determine. The buffer alternative — absorbing imbalance through inventory, idle capacity, or financial reserves — postpones the problem rather than resolving it.

What Shaikh (2016) establishes is therefore not just that capitalism is unstable. It is that capitalism is unstable in a way that cannot be corrected by the firms whose decisions produce the instability. The problem of stabilization is real, and it sits outside the firm's reach.

The mechanism that takes up this work cannot be reduced to the firm itself, nor can it be assembled from the aggregation of firm-level decisions. It must operate at a different scale — the scale at which the cumulative effects of decentralized investment can be observed, at which the buffers absorbing imbalance can be replenished or extended, at which the institutional capacity to redirect resources exists. Whether this scale is the national economy, the international monetary system, the regional supply chain, or some combination of these is a historical question, not a theoretical one. The forms vary across time and place. What does not vary is the requirement: stabilization that does not occur internally to the firm must occur somewhere, in some institutional form, or it does not occur at all. The question is what mechanism, at what scale, and through what institutional form, takes up the work of stabilizing what the supply side itself cannot.

Markets are supposed to coordinate firm decisions at the macro level. Okishio (1964/2022) argues that they do not. Markets register the failure of coordination after the fact, and they do so at a level of aggregation where individual firms cannot recognize the pattern they are collectively producing. What appears in the aggregate as equilibration appears at the level of individual firms as disproportionality, timing failures, and recurrent misdirection of capital.

The structure of the argument is straightforward. Capitalist accumulation proceeds through decentralized investment decisions. Each firm decides what to produce, at what scale, with what techniques, in pursuit of profit. There is no central planner allocating capital across sectors, no coordinating mechanism that ensures the expansion of capacity in one sector matches the requirements of others. The composition of investment is determined by what individual firms expect about future demand, future prices, future technological possibilities. These expectations are formed under uncertainty, with information that is necessarily partial, and with the temporal horizon of profit calculation rather than reproductive coherence.

The result is disproportionality. Some sectors expand faster than others. Some products are produced in volumes that exceed effective demand at prevailing prices; others are produced in volumes that fall short. The relative prices that should signal the imbalance arrive with delay, and they signal it after capital has already been committed. By the time a firm recognizes that its investment was misjudged, the capital is sunk, the workers are hired, the equipment is installed. The firm faces a choice between operating at a loss, scrapping the capital, or holding capacity idle in the hope that conditions will improve. None of these choices restores proportionality. They redistribute the cost of disproportionality across firms, workers, and creditors.

This is not a contingent failure. It is the form in which capitalist accumulation proceeds. The anarchy of decentralized production and the lawful regularity of value relations are not opposites; they are the same process at different levels of analysis. The lawfulness operates at the level of the value form — the requirement that commodities exchange at ratios reflecting their socially necessary labor times, the tendency of profit rates to equalize across sectors, the long-run equilibration of supply and demand. The anarchy operates at the level of concrete production — the divergence of individual investment decisions from any aggregate coherence, the recurrent crises through which equilibration is imposed by force of devaluation rather than achieved through coordination.

Concretely: a firm in steel investing in expanded blast furnace capacity makes a bet on how much steel construction, machinery production, and durable manufacturing will demand over the period required to amortize the investment. Each of those sectors makes its own bets about its own demand, which depends in part on what is happening in steel. The bets are not independent. They condition one another. But they are made independently, by firms whose information about each other's intentions is at best partial and whose strategic incentives discourage full disclosure even where information could in principle be shared. The investments proceed without coordination, and the disproportionalities they produce are not the deviation from a coordinated equilibrium that did not happen. They are the form the absence of coordination takes. The price signal that eventually registers the imbalance arrives after the capital is committed; the response to the signal generates new commitments that will register their own imbalances in the next period; the cycle continues without convergence.

Market anarchy is not an external feature of capitalism. It is internal to how the value form operates. This internal anarchy appears at two levels simultaneously: as the requirement of value-form coordination through price equalization, and as the production-level disproportionality that such coordination perpetually fails to prevent.

The standard response to coordination failure fails: firms cannot compensate through internal planning. A firm can plan its own production. It can forecast its own demand. It can adjust its own capacity in response to its own information. What it cannot do is coordinate its decisions with those of other firms whose investments determine the conditions under which its plans will succeed or fail. The firm operates within a system of decentralized decisions, and its own planning, however sophisticated, is one element of the disproportionality it confronts. Internal coordination compounds external anarchy; it does not resolve it.

This is where the theoretical commitments of much heterodox economics begin to show strain. Theories that treat the firm as a planning entity capable of stabilizing its capacity utilization at a desired or normal level implicitly assume that firms have the information, the foresight, and the autonomy to do so. They do not. The firm is constrained by what other firms have decided, by what state agencies have allowed or restricted, by what financial institutions have lent or refused. The firm's plan is a hypothesis about an environment whose stability depends on factors the firm does not control.

The consequence is that capacity utilization, as observed at the level of any individual firm, is not the outcome of a plan executed under stable conditions. It is the outcome of a plan executed under conditions that the plan itself helps to destabilize. The firm produces excess capacity, or operates at strained capacity, not because it has chosen to but because the conditions in which it operates have shifted between the moment the capacity was installed and the moment of its use. The relationship between intended utilization and actual utilization is mediated by everything the firm does not control.

What Okishio identifies is therefore a problem markets cannot solve, that firms cannot solve through internal planning, and that has no general technical fix. Capitalist accumulation produces coordination failure as a regular feature of its operation. The question that follows is the one Okishio leaves open. If markets fail to coordinate, and firms cannot compensate through planning, what is it that firms actually do when they confront the disproportionality their own decisions help to produce?

What Okishio (1964/2022) identifies at the level of markets is a problem he cannot solve at the level of firms. Decentralized capitalist accumulation fails to coordinate investment decisions across sectors. The result is disproportionality: too much capacity here, insufficient demand there, uneven rhythms of production that no single actor orchestrates and no collective mechanism corrects. This much is clear. What is less clear — and what Okishio leaves unresolved — is what firms actually do.

The standard move is to assume firms plan. Markets fail to coordinate because they are anarchic; firms succeed because they are organized. Internal planning compensates for external chaos. Management decides, workers execute, capital is deployed toward its optimal use. The unified firm is the implicit condition of this picture: a single rational agent capable of converting market signals into coherent strategy.

The assumption is false. Not because firms lack management. Because management itself is divided.

The firm is not a unified agent. This is not because firms lack management. It is because management itself is divided. Vidal's (2022) central argument is that the labor process—the actual coordination of production—cannot be reduced to the valorization process—the extraction of surplus-value from deployed labor power. These are not merely different names for the same activity. They are constitutively in tension. To extract maximum surplus-value, management must standardize, monitor, and intensify labor. But to maintain the productivity on which that surplus depends, management must also develop labor's capacities, exercise discretion, and preserve the tacit knowledge embedded in working practice. Standardization and skill development are not complementary. They pull in opposite directions, and management must pursue both simultaneously. This is not a technical problem awaiting the right solution. It is a structural contradiction that management cannot escape.

This contradiction reappears in every dimension along which labor is organized within the firm, and it multiplies rather than resolves as management seeks more sophisticated techniques to manage it. The attempt to standardize labor—to specify tasks in measurable detail—captures only what can be quantified. To extract maximum surplus-value, management seeks tasks that can be specified in detail, monitored for compliance, and intensified through pressure or pacing. The more standardized the task, the easier its supervision; the easier its supervision, the lower the cost of extracting effort. But standardization has a cost. Tasks that are fully specified leave no space for the worker's judgment, which means that any deviation from anticipated conditions—a defective input, an unusual customer request, a malfunction in the equipment—interrupts production and waits for managerial intervention. The fully specified labor process produces workers who do exactly what they are told and nothing more. In conditions of variability, this is not productive. Management therefore preserves some scope for worker discretion, which means accepting some loss of supervisory control. The balance is local and temporary; but the contradiction returns with each shift in conditions. Management does not solve the problem. It manages it until it cannot.

The same tension reappears in performance measurement. To assess whether labor is producing the surplus-value it should, management installs systems of quantification: output per hour, defect rates, response times, customer ratings. The systems require workers to behave in measurable ways, which means standardizing the activities being measured. But the activities that are most valuable are often those that resist measurement—anticipating problems, supporting colleagues, adjusting to non-standard requests. Measurement systems push workers away from these activities and toward the metrics. Productivity rises in the measured dimensions and falls in the unmeasured ones. Management then refines the metrics to capture more, which generates new dimensions in which workers redirect effort, which generates further refinement. The system is recursive and never settles. Management chases the problem into new dimensions, pursuing it without resolution.

The contradiction takes yet another form in authority structures. The valorization imperative pushes management toward command structures in which decisions flow downward and execution upward. The production imperative pushes management toward consultative structures in which workers contribute information that management does not have. Most firms operate with hybrid structures that try to do both. The hybrid is inherently unstable: workers consulted about decisions resist being commanded about execution, and workers commanded about execution withhold information when consulted about decisions. The structure works only as long as this negotiation holds, but the negotiation itself consumes managerial attention and worker effort. When the strain accumulates faster than it can be absorbed, the hybrid collapses. Management cannot escape the contradiction. It can only temporarily organize it.

What Vidal identifies is the recognition that no version of firm organization escapes these contradictions. The firm is not a problem awaiting the right technique. It is a site where contradictory imperatives generate institutional forms whose function is not to optimize but to mediate. Management is the mediation. It does not resolve the contradiction; it manages its costs across time, distributing strain in ways that allow production to continue. When the strain accumulates faster than it can be dissipated, the firm enters a phase of dysfunction — strikes, quality crises, turnover spikes, productivity declines — that requires reorganization. The reorganization does not eliminate the contradiction. It shifts the balance among offsetting mechanisms.

Workers are not passive in this mediation. Through their daily decisions about how much effort to apply, what information to share, what discretion to exercise, what limits to enforce, they shape the conditions under which management operates. Workers observe the contradictions of managerial authority. They recognize that the same management demanding productivity increases is also demanding cost reductions that undermine productivity. They respond accordingly—not always through open conflict, but through the quiet accumulation of effort withheld, through limits on discretionary cooperation, through the calibration of work intensity to a level sustainable under contradictory pressure. Labor resistance is not an external disturbance to the firm's normal functioning. It is the normal functioning of a firm that cannot present coherent authority.

This calibration produces the configurations of capacity utilization that the firm reports. A firm operating at 75 percent of installed capacity is not running at 75 percent because it has chosen this rate as optimal. It is running at 75 percent because the workers, the equipment, the management systems, and the demand environment together produce that outcome under the contradictory pressures all of them face. A different balance among those pressures would produce a different rate. The rate is not a parameter the firm sets; it is a configuration the firm settles into, and the configuration reflects these unresolved tensions, not the firm's optimization. The configurations are not all alike. A firm operating in a sector with stable demand and a workforce accustomed to its production rhythms may settle into a configuration with relatively high utilization, modest buffer stocks, and routine management. A firm operating with volatile demand and a contested labor relation may settle into a configuration with substantial idle capacity, large inventory buffers, and management absorbed by the negotiation of every shift change. The configurations express different settlements among the same underlying contradictions, conditioned by the specific environment in which the firm operates. To say a firm is operating at 75 percent capacity utilization tells us little until we know what the firm produces, who its workers are, what its demand environment looks like, and what conditions structure its access to inputs and markets. The number is the visible surface of an institutional configuration whose substance lies elsewhere—in the labor relations, the supply networks, the regulatory environment, the financial pressures that together determine what the firm can do with the capacity it has installed.

The implication is direct. Capacity utilization cannot be modeled as a firm-level optimization variable, because the firm does not optimize. It manages a set of contradictions that have no optimal resolution. Capacity utilization can be modeled as the institutional outcome of how those contradictions are mediated under specific historical conditions — conditions that include the structure of demand, the organization of labor, the technologies available, the regulatory environment, and the financial pressures the firm operates under. None of these conditions is static. All of them shift across time, and the firm's utilization configurations shift with them.

Capacity utilization is therefore a historical object, not a behavioral parameter. It expresses a settlement among forces, not a choice among alternatives. The settlement holds for as long as the forces hold; when one of them shifts substantially — a new technology, a labor revolt, a financial crisis, a state intervention — the settlement breaks down and a new configuration emerges. The new configuration is not better or worse in some absolute sense. It is a different mediation of the same underlying contradictions, organized under different conditions.

Capacity utilization, in other words, is not a choice. It is the configuration through which these contradictions are managed.

What Okishio identifies at the level of markets — the disproportionality produced by decentralized accumulation, the failure of price signals to coordinate investment, the impossibility of internal planning compensating for external anarchy — does not disappear when capital is internalized within the firm. The firm is not a refuge from market anarchy. It is the institutional site where market anarchy reappears, transformed but not resolved, as the contradiction between valorization and labor process, between standardization and discretion, between measured performance and the activities that resist measurement.

Marxist political economy has long distinguished two modes of capital's domination: the direct subordination of labor to management at the point of production, and the impersonal subordination of all economic actors — workers and capitalists alike — to the imperatives of the value form operating through markets (Basu, 2022). The two have often been treated as complementary but distinct: the firm-level domination accounting for what happens within production, the market-level domination accounting for what happens between firms.

The framework reconstructed across Okishio and Vidal undoes this separation. Market anarchy in the value form is not the external environment within which the firm's internal contradictions unfold. It is the mediated source of those contradictions. The firm's divided management is the institutional form through which market anarchy is internalized, routed through the labor process, and absorbed into configurations of capacity utilization that hold the contradiction in suspension without resolving it. Conversely, the firm's contradictions are not local pathologies that better management could correct. They are the form of appearance of the value form's anarchy at the site of production.

These are not two anarchies. There is one anarchy, internal to capitalist accumulation, and it appears at two levels: as the disproportionality and coordination failure of decentralized investment, and as the contradictory imperatives of management divided  in the labour process,  provoke labor resistance within the firm. The two levels are inseparable. Each generates the conditions under which the other operates. Each absorbs the strain the other cannot.

The site of theorization for political economy is therefore not a firm-level normal degree of capacity utilization. There is no normal degree. There is no equilibrium configuration toward which firms tend in the absence of disturbance. There is the historically specific, contradictory configuration through which firms manage the simultaneous pressure of market anarchy and labor resistance, under conditions that are themselves the institutional sediment of past mediations of the same contradictions. What this configuration is in any concrete case is a question of historical conditions. To understand it requires understanding the specific demand environment, the specific organization of labor, the specific role of the state, the specific position of the firm within the broader division of labor. The configuration is historical, contextual, and contradictory. It is not a parameter. It is an institutional settlement.

A firm attempting to industrialize in the periphery faced a structural contradiction: it needed advanced machinery to be competitive, but that machinery could not be produced domestically. Industrialization through import substitution created the appearance of autarky—domestic production replacing imported consumer goods—but it did not reduce the dependence on external capital goods sources. Instead, it relocated that dependence. Instead of importing finished goods, the periphery now imported the means of production. The foreign exchange required to do so oscillated with commodity cycles and world prices beyond the periphery's control. This structural dependence on capital goods imports created a non-reinvestment problem: a peripheral firm could not automatically reinvest accumulated profit in expanded capacity because the import depended on foreign exchange availability and state allocation, which were externally determined. 

The firm had to choose between reinvesting at the pace the import cycle allowed or accumulating surplus in financial forms—cash reserves, liquid assets held against the moments when imports became available. This non-reinvestment of surplus replicates inside the firm the contradiction that Vidal (2022) identified: the firm cannot present to its workers a coherent accumulation strategy; management must simultaneously demand lower wages and higher intensity (cost containment under constraint) and demand the flexibility and discretionary effort that expansion would require; labor responds by withholding effort the firm cannot coherently claim. The balance of payments becomes the medium through which external constraint channels into internal pressure: foreign exchange scarcity that prevents capital goods imports becomes the pressure preventing utilization of installed capacity, which becomes the pressure constraining wages, which becomes labor resistance preventing management coordination. The firm's capacity utilization configuration is therefore the institutional settlement through which it manages not the abstract contradiction between markets and planning, but the concrete contradiction between technological dependence and financial constraint imposed by its structural position in the world capitalist division of labor.

The capacity utilization configurations that emerge from peripheral structural position are not timeless; they are historically specific settlements shaped by how particular state forms attempt to mediate the contradiction between technological dependence and financial constraint. Import substitution as a development strategy took a specific institutional form in the 1950s and 1960s: tariff protection for domestic industry, state credit allocation toward capital goods imports, exchange controls directing foreign exchange toward selected sectors, with institutional forms varying across cases (development corporations, central banks, directed credit systems) but the logic consistent—the state would compensate for the market's structural inability to coordinate capital goods allocation by managing the flow of capital goods and foreign exchange. This settlement operated through differential integration into the world market: the core maintained direct control over technological innovation and capital goods production while the periphery specialized in primary commodity production and import-substituting industries consuming core technology; the terms of trade systematically favored the core, and capital goods prices rose relative to primary commodity prices, creating what Prebisch (1981) identified as technological dependency—the periphery must continuously export more to import equivalent machinery, yet the foreign exchange constraint never disappears. Development appeared as an impossible chase: accumulate faster, export more, import more machinery, increase productivity, yet never escape the external constraint that defined peripheral position. The feasibility of such settlements depended on specific historical conditions: sufficient commodity exports to generate foreign exchange, sufficient state capacity to implement exchange controls, sufficient labor organization to be incorporated rather than suppressed, sufficient Cold War context tolerating developmental state experimentation. The capacity utilization configurations we observe are therefore the institutional expressions through which particular developmental states attempted to mediate contradictions arising from peripheral position within a capitalist world economy, where neither firm-level optimization nor market-coordinated adjustment is available. What remains analytically is understanding how the transformation elasticity θ — the institutional mediation through which accumulation is converted into productive capacity — operates differently across these differentiated structural positions, and how the crisis of these settlements emerges from the same world-historical forces that fractured the Atlantic Fordist core.

The framework reconstructed across this section — market anarchy in its value form, divided management as its form of appearance inside the firm, contradictory utilization configurations as the institutional settlement through which the contradiction is managed — is not specific to peripheral capitalism. It is the general framework through which capitalist accumulation operates. What is specific to peripheral capitalism is the configuration: the particular conditions under which the contradictions are mediated, the particular constraints on what mediation is possible, the particular forms that the settlement takes.

This specification matters because it sets up the comparative analysis that the dissertation requires. The crisis of Atlantic Fordism cannot be analyzed as a national event in the United States and Western Europe whose effects then propagated outward to a periphery that had been operating on a different logic. The center and the periphery were not operating on different logics. They were operating on the same logic — the same anarchy of decentralized accumulation, the same divided management, the same labor resistance — under different conditions, mediated through different institutional configurations, producing different historical settlements. When those settlements broke down, they broke down together, because the conditions that allowed each to hold were entangled with the conditions that allowed the others to hold.

A comparative-relational analysis of crisis takes this entanglement as its starting point. It asks how the configurations that stabilized accumulation in the center — military expenditure as a permanent component of demand, credit-financed mass consumption, dollar hegemony as an instrument of external adjustment — depended on configurations in the periphery that allowed strategic raw materials to flow at administered prices, that absorbed manufactured exports through protected markets, that recycled surplus through financial channels the center controlled. And it asks how the configurations that stabilized accumulation in the periphery — import substitution, state-directed credit, foreign exchange management — depended on a center whose own stabilization permitted those configurations to operate.

Section 3 turns to the hinge object — the transformation elasticity θ — that allows this articulation to be specified empirically. What is at stake in θ is not a parameter to be estimated. It is the institutional mediation through which accumulation is converted into productive capacity, and through which that conversion proceeds differently across the structural positions that the world capitalist system distributes.

## Empirical Strategy

### 1. Object of identification: the transformation elasticity

Capital accumulation does not become productive capacity by itself. The conversion is mediated. The empirical object is that mediation: the transformation elasticity, $\theta$, linking accumulation to productive-capacity growth. $\theta$ is not a behavioral parameter chosen by firms; empirically, it is recovered as the transformation elasticity that condenses historically specific mediations. Without identifying $\theta$, productive capacity remains unconstructed, and utilization remains assumed, residualized, or normalized without an object.

The empirical strategy does not estimate the firm-level configuration directly. It estimates the macro relation in which those configurations appear after being stabilized, aggregated, and historically sedimented into the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity.

Distribution enters this relation through the wage share. It conditions the mechanization path, the cost-minimizing composition of capital, and the organizational form through which accumulation becomes capacity (Cajas Guijarro, 2024). The sequence is:

$$
\omega_t \rightarrow \theta_t \rightarrow Y_t^p \rightarrow u_t
$$

not:

$$
\omega_t \rightarrow u_t
$$

Formally:

$$
\hat{y}_t^p = \theta(\omega_t)\hat{k}_t
$$

where $\omega_t$ is the wage share, $\theta(\omega_t)$ is the transformation elasticity, $\hat{k}_t$ is capital-stock growth, and $\hat{y}_t^p$ is productive-capacity growth. The object is not accumulation alone, but the mediated relation through which accumulation becomes capacity.

Realized output follows effective demand (Keynes, 1936):

$$
Y_t = C_t + I_t + G_t + X_t - M_t
$$

This determines actual output, not productive capacity. Utilization can be recovered only after the productive-capacity path has been reconstructed:

$$
u_t = \frac{Y_t}{Y_t^p}
$$

Without the transformation relation, utilization has no empirical object.

### 2. Construction logic: observed and recovered objects

Output, capital, and distribution are observed. Productive capacity is not. The reconstruction follows a strict sequence:

$$
\text{long-run transformation relation}
\rightarrow
\hat{\theta}_t
\rightarrow
\hat{Y}_t^p
\rightarrow
\hat{u}_t
$$

The central long-run relation is:

$$
y_t
=
c
+
\beta_1 k_t
+
\beta_2(\omega_t k_t)
+
\xi_t
$$

with:

$$
\theta_t=\beta_1+\beta_2\omega_t
$$

The equation identifies the elasticity through which productive capacity can be reconstructed. It does not define utilization. The interaction term is read as structural mediation only within the observed support of the wage share and only where the long-run relation is stable across coherent windows.

The recovered $\theta_t$ builds the productive-capacity path:

$$
Y_t^p
=
A_{t_0}
\exp\left(
\sum_{\tau=t_0}^{t}
\theta_\tau \Delta \ln K_\tau
\right)
$$

Utilization is then derived:

$$
u_t=\frac{Y_t}{Y_t^p}
$$

Super-consistency matters at the first step. It strengthens recovery of the long-run coefficient vector under integration. It does not recover the object. Coefficient consistency does not imply object consistency.

### 3. Data and measurement

Comparability is achieved through common accounting architecture, not through identical statistical systems. The United States enters through direct-source reconstruction. Chile enters through harmonization.

Capital-stock construction is identification-relevant. Productive capacity requires a capital-stock measure compatible with the transformation relation. Profitability also requires a distinction between gross and net stocks. Gross capital stock refers to capital still in operation. Net capital stock refers to written-down value. Depreciation governs value attrition. Retirement governs physical exit.

The shared construction logic is a generalized perpetual-inventory method:

$$
K^{GR}_{i,t}
=
IG^R_{i,t}
+
(1-\rho_{i,t})K^{GR}_{i,t-1}
$$

where $IG^R_{i,t}$ is gross real investment and $\rho_{i,t}$ is the retirement rate. The recursion is shared. The source pathways are not.

The U.S. backend is organized around BEA income and fixed-assets materials. Its role is to provide the direct-source construction backend for U.S. capital, income, and fixed-asset objects.

Chile is reconstructed through three surfaces: a frozen Hofman reference, a canonical Pérez baseline, and an additive BCCh extension. The canonical baseline uses Pérez FBKF excluding inventories in constant 2003 CLP for 1900–1994. The BCCh extension carries the series forward to 2024 while preserving the historical Pérez segment. ClioLab and BCCh total real FBKF remain diagnostic sources, not primary production inputs.

The main text keeps only what identification needs: constructed capital stocks, the depreciation/retirement distinction, gross and net analytical roles, and the rule that comparability rests on accounting consistency. Source reconciliation, splice diagnostics, retirement assumptions, service-life parameters, and repository provenance move to the appendix.

### 4. Negative benchmark: mechanization frontier and cost-minimizing path

The mechanization frontier supplies the missing benchmark, not an observed firm problem. Following Cajas Guijarro (2024), labor-productivity growth is written as a function of mechanization growth:

$$
\hat a = g(\hat q)
$$

The benchmark condition is:

$$
g_{\hat q}(\hat q^*(\omega)) = 1-\omega
$$

This identifies the cost-minimizing mechanization path. Distribution enters because the wage share changes the cost condition under which mechanization is selected. The transformation elasticity implied by that path is:

$$
\theta(\omega)
=
\frac{
g(\hat q^*(\omega))
}{
\hat q^*(\omega)
}
$$

The benchmark is not an observed capacity denominator. It is the missing case under which capacity would already be coherent. The empirical strategy begins from its absence: $\theta_t$, productive capacity, and utilization must be recovered.

### 5. Central specification and estimator hierarchy

The central specification recovers the distributive mediation of the transformation relation:

$$
\ln Y_t
=
\alpha
+
\beta_1 \ln K_t
+
\beta_2(\omega_t \ln K_t)
+
\varepsilon_t
$$

with:

$$
\theta_t=\beta_1+\beta_2\omega_t
$$

Here $\beta_1$ captures the baseline capacity-building power of accumulation. $\beta_2$ captures the distributive mediation of that power. The interaction term is not a free-floating correlation. It is the formal proxy for the distributive conditioning of the accumulation–capacity nexus.

FM-OLS is preferred. IM-OLS checks robustness. DOLS is excluded.

DOLS is excluded because its dynamic augmentation requires leads and lags of first differences. With $k_t\omega_t$ as the key regressor, differencing produces:

$$
\Delta(k_t\omega_t)
=
\Delta k_t \omega_t
+
k_t \Delta \omega_t
$$

These auxiliary terms have no structural interpretation in the reconstruction of $\theta_t$. DOLS would correct the regression by adding variables that are not part of the object.

FM-OLS corrects the long-run regression for endogeneity and serial correlation through long-run covariance adjustments. IM-OLS provides an alternative modified-OLS route based on an integrated transformation of the regression model. The comparison is judged through coefficient stability, standard errors, observed wage-share support, and the implied paths of $\theta_t$, productive capacity, and utilization.

### 6. Peripheral extension: external mediation of the transformation relation

Center and periphery are not norm and deviation. They are different mediations of the same object. Accumulation must be transformed into productive capacity in both cases. The object is the same. The mediation is not.

In the central case, the transformation relation is more internally mediated. In Chile, the same relation is externally constrained by foreign exchange, imported means of production, external balance, financial dependence, income flows, and capital composition. These conditions are not disturbances around a central norm. They shape the conditions under which accumulation can become capacity.

Kaldor (1959) does not extend the mechanization frontier. His intervention on Chile provides the peripheral problem that makes the extension necessary: accumulation depends on imported means of production and foreign exchange, while surplus use, luxury consumption, and technological dependence displace internal contradictions toward the balance of payments.

This chapter uses that diagnosis to extend the Cajas Guijarro mechanization logic toward the analytical identification of an effective profit share under a binding external constraint.

The peripheral extension allows $\theta_t$ to vary with both distributive and external conditions:

$$
\ln Y_t
=
\alpha
+
\beta_1 \ln K_t
+
\beta_2(\omega_t \ln K_t)
+
\beta_3(z_t \ln K_t)
+
\beta_4(\omega_t z_t \ln K_t)
+
\varepsilon_t
$$

so that:

$$
\theta_t
=
\beta_1
+
\beta_2\omega_t
+
\beta_3z_t
+
\beta_4\omega_t z_t
$$

Here $z_t$ denotes the peripheral conditioning variable: terms of trade, external leverage, import capacity, reserve stress, or another balance-of-payments state. In the periphery, accumulation becomes productive capacity through a transformation relation conditioned by both distribution and the external corridor.

### 7. Normalization and scope of inference

The recovered capacity path requires a level anchor. A pinch year supplies that anchor by imposing:

$$
u_{t_0}=1
$$

at a year interpreted as approximate frontier or full-capacity operation. The pinch year anchors the level of utilization. It does not define utilization. The definition comes from the structurally identified transformation relation.

Alternative pinch years test level sensitivity, not the definition of the object. If the reconstructed series changes only in level while preserving the historical pattern, the normalization is doing its work. If the interpretation depends on a single pinch year, the reconstruction is not yet stable.

The long-run transformation relation is identifiable under the maintained cointegration conditions. The reconstruction of $u_t$ remains conditional on the recovered $\theta_t$, the productive-capacity translation, and the level normalization. The regime-dependent peripheral extension remains a separate identification problem when the external state is specified as a threshold or historically bounded corridor.

Empirical identification targets the transformation relation directly. Utilization is reconstructed only downstream.
