# From Okishio → Vidal → Transformation Elasticity

## Core claim

The mechanization problem does not require a unified firm solving a profit-maximization problem.

It can be grounded instead in:

> decentralized accumulation (Okishio) + internally divided firm (Vidal)

Under this view:

- market anarchy is not resolved by the firm  
- it is internalized inside the firm  
- mechanization emerges as a form of appearance of value under contradiction  

---

## 1. Okishio — decentralized accumulation and market anarchy

Okishio’s contribution is not simply instability.

It is a **form of coordination**:

> accumulation is decentralized, privately organized, and validated only ex post through the market

This generates:

- disproportionality  
- disequilibrium  
- idle capacity  
- crisis tendencies

$$
\text{decentralized accumulation}
\rightarrow
\text{disproportionality}
\rightarrow
\text{macro disequilibrium}
\rightarrow
\text{idle capacity}
\rightarrow
\text{crisis}
$$

## 2. The unified-firm gap

Okishio’s framework implicitly assumes:

> firms respond as coherent agents to profitability and demand signals

This is analytically powerful but incomplete.

The gap:

> how is market anarchy processed inside the firm?

:contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}  

---

## 3. Vidal — internally divided firm

The firm is not a unified optimizer.

It is:

- divided across scales (shop floor, plant, corporate, finance)  
- governed by conflicting objectives  
- structured by labor–management contradiction  
- organized through contested capacity conventions  

So:

> the firm does not solve market anarchy  
> it reorganizes it internally  

---

## 4. Reinterpretation of mechanization

Under the unified-firm assumption:

$$
\min_q \left[(1-\omega)q - a(q)\right]
$$

is read as:

> a coherent cost-minimization problem

Under the Okishio–Vidal articulation, this changes meaning.

Mechanization is:

- not a single decision  
- not globally optimal  
- not necessarily consistent  

It becomes:

> an outcome of internally mediated responses to market anarchy  

---

## 5. Mechanization as form of appearance

Mechanization is reinterpreted as:

> a form of appearance of value under decentralized accumulation and internal contradiction

This means:

- different parts of the firm respond differently  
- mechanization may be excessive, insufficient, or mistimed  
- productivity gains are uneven and contested  
- capacity expansion is not coordinated  

Thus:

$$
q_t
$$

is not an optimal choice variable in a strict sense.

It is:

> the realized outcome of contradictory internal processes under market pressure  

---

## 6. Implication for transformation elasticity

The transformation elasticity:

$$
\theta_t^N
=
1+\frac{a_t-q_t}{g_{K,t}^N}
$$

does not emerge from:

> an optimal allocation of resources

It emerges from:

- decentralized accumulation  
- internally fractured firm behavior  
- uneven mechanization responses  
- constrained recapitalization  

So:

$$
a_t - q_t
$$

is not a smooth technical relation.

It is:

> the macro outcome of internally contradictory adjustment processes  

---

## 7. Re-reading the Cajas hinge

Cajas provides:

$$
q^*(\omega)
$$

as a clean optimization rule.

Under the Okishio–Vidal articulation:

- this becomes a **limiting case**  
- not a literal behavioral assumption  

Interpretation:

> the Cajas solution represents a coherent benchmark  
> the actual economy deviates from it through internal contradiction

## 10. Empirical Reconciliation (The Identification Bridge)

The empirical identification strategy (FM-OLS estimation of the capacity envelope) does **not** assume a unified, profit-maximizing firm. Instead, the econometric model is interpreted as a **macro-structural envelope of contradictory historical realizations**.

**Mapping the Empirical Specification to the Ontology:**
1. **The Capital Stocks ($k^{nrc}_t, k^{me}_t$):** Represent the sunk, physical legacy of decentralized accumulation, not optimal long-run demand.
2. **The Distributive Interaction ($\omega_t \cdot k^{me}_t$):** Acts as the **Vidal Friction Term**. It measures the contested, uneven productivity payoff of machinery under wage pressure, reflecting internal firm division rather than smooth substitution.
3. **The External Interaction ($E_t \cdot k^{me}_t$):** Acts as the **Peripheral Wedge**, capturing the macro-disequilibrium imposed by import dependence.
4. **The Utilization Gap ($\tilde{\mu}_t$):** Serves as the empirical fingerprint of **Okishio’s Market Anarchy**. The fluctuation of $\mu_t$ around normal capacity is the direct measure of disproportionality and idle capacity generated by uncoordinated accumulation.
5. **Regime Shifts (Threshold-FGLS):** The necessity of structural breaks in the empirical model proves that the Cajas optimization ($q^*(\omega)$) is merely a limiting case. The coefficients shift when the "contested capacity conventions" (Vidal) are violently renegotiated at the macro level.

**Conclusion:** The econometrics do not estimate a neoclassical production function; they measure the *scars* of market anarchy and internal contradiction.

## Locked formulation

The mechanization problem is not fundamentally an optimization problem of a unified firm. It is the outcome of decentralized accumulation interacting with internally divided firm structures. Okishio explains the macro-level coordination failure; Vidal explains its internalization within the firm. The transformation elasticity therefore captures not an optimal mapping, but the historically realized outcome of contradictory mechanization and accumulation processes.
