# Marx–Okishio closure logic

## Core rule

The empirical strategy does not directly test the Marx–Okishio theorem.

It operationalizes Marx–Okishio closure logic at the macro-regime level.

The object is not the profit rate itself. The object is the transformation elasticity of accumulation into productive capacity.

$$
\theta(\omega)=\beta_1+\beta_2\omega
$$

## Interpretation of \(\beta_2\)

$$
\beta_2<0
$$

indicates Marx-side dominance: wage-share pressure, strong labor capture, capital-using mechanization, or compression of the normal profit-recapitalization corridor weakens the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity.

$$
\beta_2>0
$$

indicates Okishio-side dominance: wage-share pressure is associated with productivity-enhancing mechanization, accumulation-supporting technical change, or weak-labor conditions that prevent productivity gains from becoming a falling-profit tendency.

$$
\beta_2\simeq 0
$$

indicates either corridor offsetting or excessive compression in the linear specification.

## Regime hypotheses

$$
H_1:\quad \beta_2^{Fordism}<0
$$

Fordism is interpreted as a strong-labor closure.

$$
H_2:\quad \beta_2^{PostFordism}>\beta_2^{Fordism}
$$

Post-Fordism is interpreted as a weak-labor closure.

The stronger empirical possibility is:

$$
H_{2b}:\quad \beta_2^{PostFordism}>0
$$

but this is not required by the theoretical foundation.

## Guardrail

Do not write:

> This is a macro-test of the Marx–Okishio theorem.

Write instead:

> This is a regime-level empirical operationalization of Marx–Okishio closure logic through the wage-share coefficient in the transformation elasticity.
