s## Paper-facing Chapter 2 — constitutional core

### Governing problem

Capacity utilization has been treated either as a given macro ratio, an inherited benchmark, or an empirical residual. That leaves unresolved the problem of how accumulation is transformed into productive capacity under capitalist disequilibrium.

### Central claim

Capacity utilization is not a residual. It is a **structurally reconstructed object**, recovered through the identification of the transformation elasticity θ\thetaθ, where distribution mediates the conversion of accumulation into productive capacity.

### Comparative claim

The center and the periphery are not compared as norm and deviation. They are distinct determinations of the same transformation problem under different structural conditions.

### Bounded claim

The chapter does not deliver the full crisis theory, the full dependency deployment, or the full profitability system. It reconstructs the capacity-utilization problem strongly enough to support a center–periphery comparison.

That is the constitution.




# Chapter 2 — Constitutional Statement

Capacity utilization is not taken as a given ratio, nor as a residual left after fitting output to capital. It is reconstructed as a structural object emerging from the transformation of accumulation into productive capacity under capitalist disequilibrium.

The starting point is the instability of supply-side reproduction under unbalanced growth. The benchmark of smooth reproduction is retained as a formal condition of feasibility, but not as an achieved tendency. Capitalist accumulation does not converge toward proportionality. It unfolds through disproportionality, and that disproportionality appears concretely as disequilibrium.

The transformation of accumulation into productive capacity is governed by an elasticity, $\theta$, which links the growth of productive capacity to the accumulation of productive capital. This elasticity is not fixed. It is endogenously determined through the organization of production, the composition of capital, and the distributive configuration within which mechanization takes place.

Distribution enters this relation through the cost-minimizing choice of technique. Wage share affects the selection of capital intensity, and therefore the relation between accumulation and capacity formation. The transformation elasticity becomes a mediated object:

$$
\omega_t \rightarrow \theta_t \rightarrow Y_t^p
$$

Capacity utilization is then defined as the relation between realized output and this reconstructed productive capacity:

$$
\mu_t = \frac{Y_t}{Y_t^p}
$$

This object is not observed directly. It is recovered through a sequence: estimation of the long-run transformation relation, recovery of $\theta_t$, reconstruction of $Y_t^p$, and only then the derivation of $\mu_t$. Utilization is therefore not a residual. It is the outcome of a prior structural identification.

This reconstruction departs from approaches that treat utilization as a deviation from a naturally realized benchmark. The benchmark is necessary, but only as a formal reference. Its non-realization is constitutive of the system. The relevant problem is not how far the economy deviates from equilibrium, but how accumulation is transformed into capacity under conditions where proportionality is not maintained.

The empirical identification follows this logic. In the central case, the transformation relation can be recovered as a stable long-run relation between output, capital, and the interaction between capital and wage share. The elasticity $\theta_t$ is obtained from this relation, and utilization is reconstructed after imposing a benchmark normalization.

In the peripheral case, the transformation is mediated by additional constraints. External balance, capital composition, and the structure of foreign dependence condition the relation between accumulation and capacity. The external constraint does not appear as a residual imbalance but as a historically observable field: trade balances, income flows, and the accumulation of foreign liabilities. These elements compress or expand the effective transformation of accumulation into productive capacity.

The comparison between center and periphery is therefore not framed as deviation from a common norm. It is framed as a difference in the way the same transformation problem is organized under distinct structural conditions. In both cases, utilization is reconstructed through the same sequence. What differs is the determination of $\theta_t$.

The result is a redefinition of the capacity-utilization problem. Utilization is not an independent driver of accumulation, nor a passive residual. It is the expression of a structurally mediated relation between accumulation and capacity, shaped by distribution, organization of production, and, in the periphery, by the external constraint.

The only remaining operational condition is the level normalization required to convert growth-based identification into an index:

$$
\mu_{t_0} = 1
$$

at a benchmark year approximating frontier utilization.

This normalization does not define utilization. It anchors it. The definition follows from the transformation relation itself.

The chapter therefore advances a reconstruction rather than an adjustment: capacity utilization is identified as a structurally mediated object, not inferred as a residual, and its empirical recovery follows from the endogenization of the transformation elasticity under conditions of capitalist disequilibrium.
